This document establishes the artificial intelligence policy of the ELTE Faculty of Informatics at faculty, institute, and department levels. It defines supported, permitted, and prohibited uses of AI and provides supplementary rules to EU Regulation 2024/1689 and Rector's Instruction 4/2025 (X.28.) on AI use in education.

§1 — Scope & Purpose

Who and What This Covers

These guidelines apply to all students, faculty, researchers, and administrative staff across all courses, degree programs (BSc, MSc, doctoral), research projects, and administrative processes within the Faculty.

§2 — Core Principles

Universal Guidelines

Principle

Human Oversight & Responsibility

AI is solely a tool. Ultimate responsibility for content, accuracy, ethical and legal compliance rests with the human user — always.

Principle

Educational Purpose

AI must support and enrich teaching and learning, not replace the learning process. Guidance on benefits, limitations, and risks must accompany its use.

Principle

Transparency

In all evaluated assignments, AI tool usage must be referenced: which tool, when, for what purpose, and in which part of the work.

Principle

Academic Integrity

AI-generated content may only appear in student submissions if the instructor explicitly grants permission. Submitted work must reflect the student's own thinking.

Principle

Ethics, Data Protection & Privacy

AI use must comply with GDPR, institutional confidential data protection, and copyright/intellectual property regulations. Sensitive, personal, or unpublished data may only be entered into external AI tools with explicit permission.

§3 — Courses & Theses

AI Use in Academic Work

Course Requirements

Instructors must publish AI usage rules on the course description page and review them each semester. The syllabus must specify:

# Required Specification
01Conditions for AI use per assignment type (prohibited / conditionally permitted / allowed / required)
02Specific AI usage rules for individual tasks, where relevant
03Student declaration and documentation requirements
04Links to applicable university and faculty regulations and the AI declaration
05Method of AI use in the evaluation of student performance (if relevant)
06Penalties for absent, missing, or falsified AI declarations

BSc & MSc Theses — Specific Rules

In all student work (theses, assignments, homework), it is mandatory to indicate AI use at the point of use (e.g., via footnote or code comment).

Required

Advisor Oversight

The thesis advisor continuously consults with the student regarding AI use and monitors its use during each phase of the thesis writing process.

Required

AI Usage Declaration

A student AI usage declaration is mandatory — included as part of the methodology chapter or developer documentation — with an overview summary for the entire work.

The Four Levels of AI Use Permission

ProhibitedStrictly no AI
All submitted work must be the student's own. Using generative AI to complete any part of the assignment is prohibited. Violations result in denial of grade or disciplinary consequences.
ConditionalLimited AI permitted
AI may be used as an aid only — for brainstorming, prototyping, drafting, or the research phase — provided it aligns with learning objectives.
  • Using AI to write the substantive content of the assignment is prohibited
  • Usage must be documented at point of use (tool name in footnote/comment)
  • Complete AI interaction log must be retained and available upon request
PermittedAI use welcomed
Generative AI tools can assist with brainstorming, literature review, drafting, code/diagram generation, or testing alternative approaches. Use is welcome and may be encouraged for certain assignments.
ExpectedAI use required
AI use is expected in practical assignments to develop solution strategies, process literature, and summarize findings. Students must independently and critically evaluate results. Proper, intended, and independent AI use is part of the assessment.
§4 — Research, Development & Innovation

AI in RDI Activities

AI may significantly enhance the efficiency of research activities; however it cannot replace scientific reasoning, AI-generated data and conclusions require critical evaluation, and ethical and legal issues may arise.

AI use in research is generally permitted (literature review, hypothesis generation, data analysis, code generation, text/literature editing, formatting), with the following obligations:

Obligation

Critical Assessment

AI-generated content must be critically assessed. Consider that AI models reflect biases in training data, outputs are nondeterministic, and AI may reproduce expressions from training data.

Obligation

Documentation

AI usage must be clearly documented in accordance with publisher, client, funding body, or corporate data protection requirements.

Prohibition

No Uploading Sensitive Research Data

Do not upload unpublished results ("raw data"), personal data of students/researchers/partners, or industrial research results to external public AI platforms. Compliance with GDPR and field-specific data management regulations is mandatory.

§7 — Consequences

Violations & Penalties

Consequences of Non-Compliance

  • Violations are handled according to the institution's general disciplinary and ethical regulations.
  • The instructor and/or responsible committee (Research Ethics Committee, Student Disciplinary Board) investigates; submitted work may be rejected or deemed unassessable.
  • Undocumented or inaccurately documented AI use, or a false AI Declaration, is treated the same as plagiarism — Academic and Examination Regulations Sections 74A–E apply.
  • In case of plagiarism, the thesis/diploma work cannot be evaluated, and disciplinary proceedings may be initiated.
  • Uploading research data subject to publication restrictions to AI systems may entail legal liability.
  • Uploading personal data without explicit consent constitutes a breach of GDPR and may result in legal or ethical accountability.

  • In case of plagiarism, your thesis/diploma work cannot be evaluated and disciplinary proceedings may be initiated
Definitions

Key Terms

Artificial Intelligence
Computer systems capable of partially or fully automating tasks requiring human interpretation, decision-making, or problem-solving — including text generation, translation, visual/audio analysis, and generative AI applications.
Generative AI (GAI)
AI systems that generate new, previously non-existent content — text, images, audio, video, or code — based on user instructions and patterns learned from large datasets.
Large Language Model
A type of generative AI trained on massive text collections, designed to generate human-like text, answer questions, and follow natural-language instructions.
Machine Learning (ML)
A subfield of AI where models are trained on data to recognize patterns and make predictions or decisions without predefined rules.
Narrow / Embedded AI
AI integrated into specific tools performing a well-defined task (e.g., spell checkers, spam filters).
General-purpose AI
AI tools applicable to a wide variety of tasks and fields (e.g., LLM-based open-ended chatbots).
Prompt
The input or instruction a user provides to an AI system (often in natural language) that determines the system's response or generated output.
Hallucination
An incorrect or unfounded response produced by an AI system, often presented as fact.
AI-assisted Work
Academic work in which AI tools were used in any part of the process (brainstorming, drafting, editing, coding, etc.), while the human author remains responsible for the final result and its integrity.
Training Data
Texts, images, code, or other data used to train, develop, and fine-tune an AI model — significantly influencing its operation, strengths, and biases.
